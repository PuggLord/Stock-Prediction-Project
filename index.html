Chapter 1 — The Problem

Stock prices look like chaos.

NVIDIA's stock went from ~$5 in 2015 to over $130 in 2024. But underneath that historic run, day-to-day movements are noisy and nearly impossible to predict.

Can a machine learning model find a signal inside that noise?

Chapter 1 — The Problem

We simplified the question.

Instead of predicting the exact price, we asked something binary:

Will NVDA close higher 3 days from now?

Each trading day gets a label: ↑ UP or ↓ DOWN. This turns a regression nightmare into a classification problem.
Chapter 2 — The Data

10 years of NVIDIA data.

We pulled daily OHLCV data from Yahoo Finance — 2015 to present — giving us over 2,500 trading days to work with.

Each day provides 6 raw values:

Chapter 3 — Feature Engineering

Raw prices aren't enough.

A model fed raw closing prices learns the trend — not the pattern. We need features that capture relative movement, not absolute levels.

So we engineered technical indicators, using only data available at the time of prediction.

Chapter 3 — Feature Engineering

Moving averages smooth the noise.

A 20-day moving average tracks short-term momentum. When price crosses above MA20, it's historically a bullish signal.

Computed as the rolling mean of the past 20 closes — purely backward-looking, no data leakage.
Chapter 3 — Feature Engineering

Layering in more signals.

The 50-day MA captures medium-term trend. The gap between MA20 and MA50 is itself a momentum signal — one of our strongest features.

We also added rolling volatility and daily return to round out the feature set.

MA20 MA50 Daily Return Volatility Momentum
Chapter 4 — The Models

Two models, two philosophies.

Our correlation analysis showed momentum indicators outperform raw volume in predictive capacity — confirming that the relationship is nonlinear. We tested two classifiers to exploit this.

Chapter 5 — Evaluation

Grading a prediction.

Accuracy alone is deceptive. A model that always predicts UP is "right" ~52% of the time — and useless.

The ROC curve plots the tradeoff between catching true UP days and generating false alarms. A perfect model hugs the top-left corner. The diagonal is a coin flip.

AUC (area under the curve) is our primary metric. Anything above 0.55 on market data is meaningful.
Chapter 6 — What We Learned

Prediction is hard.
The methodology is the point.

The pipeline never fully converged — stock prediction is genuinely difficult. Even professional quant funds struggle to beat the market consistently. The efficient market hypothesis exists for a reason.

But the process revealed something concrete: feature engineering matters more than model choice. Momentum indicators outperformed raw volume significantly, and nonlinear models like Random Forest are the right tool for this type of problem.

What we built was a rigorous, leak-proof data science pipeline — proper chronological train/test splits, no future data in features, and a principled evaluation framework. That's the foundation any real system would require.
